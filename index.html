Geographic divide
See it on the map
8 Mile Road (officially called Baseline Road) runs east-west for 26 miles, forming the municipal boundary between Detroit and its northern suburbs. It is one of the starkest visible dividing lines in American urban geography — and in American public health.

Historical context
How we got here: a timeline
The health disparities we see today did not emerge by accident. They are the direct and measurable consequence of deliberate policy decisions spanning nearly a century.
1930s — Federal Redlining
Home Owners' Loan Corporation grades Detroit neighborhoods
Federal maps color Black Detroit neighborhoods red — "hazardous" for mortgage lending. Banks refuse loans, blocking homeownership and wealth-building for generations of Black Detroiters. These same boundaries still predict health outcomes today.
1940s–50s — Restrictive Covenants & Racial Steering
Legal agreements bar Black residents from Grosse Pointe
The Grosse Pointe Property Owners Association uses a formal "point system" to screen prospective buyers by race and ethnicity, explicitly excluding Black families. This concentrates wealth on one side of 8 Mile and poverty on the other.
1950s–60s — Urban Renewal & Highway Construction
I-75 and I-375 demolish Black neighborhoods
Federal highway projects bulldoze the historic Black Bottom and Paradise Valley neighborhoods — Detroit's thriving Black business and cultural districts — displacing over 40,000 residents into already-overcrowded areas with fewer resources.
1967 — Detroit Uprising & White Flight
Accelerated suburban migration drains tax base
Following civil unrest rooted in police brutality and housing discrimination, white middle-class families rapidly leave Detroit for suburbs like Grosse Pointe. This "white flight" collapses Detroit's property tax base, defunding schools, hospitals, and public infrastructure.
1980s–90s — Deindustrialization
Auto industry decline devastates Detroit's economy
As the Big Three automakers downsize and offshore production, Detroit loses hundreds of thousands of stable, union manufacturing jobs. Unemployment and poverty spike. Vacant lots, abandoned factories, and blighted housing become defining features of the east side landscape.
2013 — Detroit Bankruptcy
City files largest municipal bankruptcy in U.S. history
Detroit files for Chapter 9 bankruptcy, triggering cuts to city services, pensions, and health benefits. Hospital closures and clinic consolidations reduce healthcare access precisely when economic hardship is at its peak. Grosse Pointe, a separate municipality, is entirely unaffected.
Today — The Accumulated Toll
Decades of policy decisions become biological reality
Researchers can trace present-day health outcomes — diabetes rates, asthma prevalence, infant mortality, life expectancy — directly back to where redlining maps drew their lines in the 1930s. The body keeps the score of political decisions made before most residents were born.
Structural racism is a public health crisis. The American Public Health Association, CDC, and WHO have all formally recognized that racism — not just individual behaviors — is a fundamental cause of health inequities. Detroit is one of its most documented examples in the United States.
Social determinants of health
Why does this happen?
Health is not just a medical issue. Up to 80% of health outcomes are shaped by the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age.
💵
Income & wealth inequality
Lower income restricts access to healthy food, safe housing, and preventive care — creating a compounding cycle of health disadvantage across generations.
🏘️
Legacy of redlining
Federal housing policies in the 1930s–60s denied mortgages to Black residents and concentrated poverty in specific neighborhoods. Detroit's east side reflects these deliberate choices.
🏪
Food environment
Detroit's east side is a documented food desert. Without reliable access to nutritious food, managing chronic conditions like diabetes and heart disease becomes significantly harder.
🏥
Healthcare access
High uninsured rates and fewer local clinics mean conditions go unmanaged longer. Emergency rooms become the primary care setting — costly and inefficient for chronic disease.
🌳
Built environment
Safe sidewalks, maintained parks, and green space support physical activity and mental health. Disinvestment in Detroit's neighborhoods removed these health-promoting infrastructure assets.
🎓
Education & employment
School quality and stable employment shape lifetime income, health literacy, and stress levels — all upstream determinants that cascade into long-term health outcomes.
The Commission on Social Determinants of Health (WHO, 2008) found that social and economic conditions are the most powerful predictors of population health — more powerful than genetics or clinical care combined. Detroit vs. Grosse Pointe is a living case study of this principle.
